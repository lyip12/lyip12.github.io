GLOBAL HUNGER INDEX VISUALIZATION

The Global Hunger Index (GHI) is designed to comprehensively measure and track hunger globally and by country and region. Calculated each year by the International Food Policy Research Institute (IFPRI), the GHI highlights successes and failures in hunger reduction and provides insights into the drivers of hunger. (IFPRI, 2015) This is a CompSci171 homework assignment.

The visualization above reflects the data from given GHI data set. The indexes are grouped by Countries, as individual countries' indexes are reflections of their unique circumstances and it does not make sense to compare them to each other. The amount of spoil (red) on the grain (shades of yellow, changing based on the year) represents the GHI data - where the greater the number, worse the situation. This visualization intends to experiment with visual motifs and understanding.

U.S. GUN DEATHS [ IN 2013 ]

https://guns.periscopic.com/?year=2013
This definitely isn't the most visually distinguishable visualizations on the web, especially considering the number of data entries. However, it is quite powerful. This is a project by Periscope on visualizing gun death information for the general public. Data on guns and deaths can be difficult to visualize, given the personal and emotional relations, yet this one has to be one of the most compelling.

  • The creators used intriguing (and very appropriate) techniques such as a motion-based walkthrough, interpolation, subtility and various visual metaphors (like yellow-warning, the trajectory of life, etc.) to convey their perspective on gun-control.

  • It is especially helpful that they have included mass-mined links to every single source of data, as well as additional information such as population sub-groups and means.

One potential improvement they could add would be additional interaction guidance on this visualization (as it is a little hard to navigate around without knowing what's possible). Otherwise, this is a rather powerful visualization.

GUN CONTROL IN AMERICA: A STATE BY STATE BREAKDOWN

https://www.theglobeandmail.com/news/world/gun-control-in-america-a-state-by-state-breakdown/article6465107/
This is a very neutral visualization on gun-control information in the US.

  • It's slightly hard to navigate and can be misleading, as the information is grouped by states, yet the presentation is in a radial-dial/pie, which suggests another type of data (%).

  • The typography is too small/indistinguishable and visual color-coding is not intuitive (other than the blue-red coding for the political parties).

  • Overall layout of the visualization makes it hard to read, with the legend and highlighted information taking to much space vertically (they could easily be combined into one), leavin the user have to scroll down to read the additional paragraphs underneath.

  • The smaller pie-charts don't mean much.

I think this is a clean visualization. It needs to consider the user experience more.

a good visualization

ROYAL CONSTELLATIONS

http://www.datasketch.es/october/code/nadieh/
Nadieh Bremer's works are always complex and beautiful. Roya Constellation is a visualization project that looks at the web of family relations of European royal and aristocratic families to see how all 10 of the current hereditary royal leaders of Europe can be connected to each other through their ancestors.

  • The key to the visualization is effective and distinguishable. Given the amount of data present, I find it surprisingly easy to find the current royals (highlighted in yellow) and their relatives through following the color-coding.

  • Nadieh has made the interaction of this really easy by implementing subtle details that makes web navigation easier than most other visualization interfaces I have encountered, such as a map-lock after click that can only be unlocked when the user moves their mouse, making scrolling and zooming in much easier than expected.

There are minor things that can be made better, such as reversing the gradients so it follows the hot-close/cold-far motif, but the overall visualization is clean, engaging and informative.

YEARS YOU HAVE LEFT TO LIVE, PROBABLY

https://flowingdata.com/2015/09/23/years-you-have-left-to-live-probably/
This one is interesting, confusing, and annoying at the same time. The graphic uses data from the Social Security Administration (likely just general age demographics information) to simulate one's possible lifetimes.

  • The constantly dropping balls is slightly triggering. It is visually interesting, yet after awhile it makes me anxious because of its dropping speed (and slow is actually really fast) and the fact that I don't feel comfortable with the association of the motion. Do they represent real individuals who's lived up to certain points, or just the ones "drop-dead" all of a sudden?

  • I don't quite understand why its a constantly running visualization. A bar graph / line graph can be easily created with less data points, but enough to present a summary of results.

  • I'm also not sure why age is important. If the data points are taken without considering when peope lived in the history, my current age shouldn't matter as much. It is at an awkward place between personal and generic with no clear definition.

I find this visualization reall interesting. What I liked was the humor of it (aka Beer, gimme) as well as the almost punch-card aesthetics, though I'm not quite sure how it contributes to the understanding of the information presented other than creating a unified mood of anxiousness.
